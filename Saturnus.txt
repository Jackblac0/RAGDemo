Saturn (symbol: ♄ ) is the sixth planet from the Sun. It is a giant planet (or Jovian planet) and the second largest planet in the Solar System. It is named after the Roman god Saturn.

Saturn is clearly flattened at the poles. Its diameter measured at the equator is almost 10 percent larger than that measured at the poles (120,536 km and 108,728 km). This is due to the planet's rapid rotation and its internal structure. Saturn is also the least dense of the planets in the solar system: its average density is only 0.69 g/cm³, which is less than that of water.

Saturn's interior is similar to Jupiter's: a silicate core surrounded by a layer of metallic liquid hydrogen. There are also signs of various ice crystals in the interior. Saturn's core is extremely hot, 12,000 kelvins, and it radiates more heat into space than it receives from the Sun.

The internal structure of Saturn
Saturn's internal structure is likely similar to that of Jupiter. Most of Saturn is liquid hydrogen and helium, with a solid rocky and iron core in the middle. The outermost are the clouds visible to us at the top of the visible atmosphere. The pressure, temperature, and density at Saturn's center are not quite as high as at Jupiter. Saturn's layer of liquid molecular hydrogen is about twice as thick as that of Jupiter[2] and extends to a depth of 30,000 kilometers. The hydrogen turns metallic quite close to the core, unlike Jupiter's. The metallic hydrogen layer is 14,000 kilometers thick. The pressure at the bottom of the atmosphere is about 100,000 bars, and at the boundary of liquid molecular hydrogen 1,000,000 bars at about half the radius of Saturn.[3][4] Some estimates suggest that Saturn has a core of ice 8,000 kilometers thick and a rocky layer 8,000 kilometers thick. The core can reach temperatures of about 12,000 Kelvin. Saturn appears to lack helium, which is thought to have fallen towards the center of the planet.source?.

Gas ring
Hydrogen, helium, and small amounts of methane and ammonia have been found in the gas ring. Winds blow at the equator in the gas ring, reaching speeds of up to 500 m/s.[5]

Saturn's yellowish cloud cover looks rather featureless from the outside, with darker and lighter stripes and fairly short-lived white spots, or whirlwinds, appearing every couple of years. A large white spot appears for a short time in the rhythm of Saturn's rotation period, about every 30 years. The storm of 1933 is the most famous. Images taken by the Cassini space probe show blue clouds similar to those on Uranus in Saturn's northern hemisphere. Saturn has a warm polar vortex, the only one known in the solar system; it is cold on other planets. A flow resembling a hexagon is visible at the north pole, a standing wave, with a hurricane-like storm in the middle around the north pole. It shows a clear eye of the storm, a high pressure. Hexagonal shapes have been produced in laboratories in computer modeling.source?.

Saturn's atmosphere is at its coldest at 0.07 bar at the surface, about 94 kelvins or −179 °C. Saturn's atmospheric chemistry is similar to Jupiter's, but because the planet's gravity is lower, the upper layer of haze is thicker and covers the lower colorful clouds. The haze is also due to the colder temperature than Jupiter's atmosphere.[6] The haze is tens of kilometers thick. The haze reflects half of the Sun's light.source?.

The stratosphere begins at the tropopause, which is at a pressure level of 0.1 atmospheres. The upper limit of ammonia clouds is 50 kilometers below this, the lower limit of ammonium hydrogen sulfide clouds is 170 kilometers below the tropopause, and water ice clouds are about 230 kilometers deeper. Saturn's rings
Main article: Rings of Saturn
Saturn's enormous rings are its most famous feature. Saturn has seven named main rings. The last new ring was discovered in 2009.[8] Galileo Galilei first observed them with his telescope in 1610, but he apparently was at a loss to say what to make of the observation. He wrote: "Saturn is not alone, but consists of three bodies, almost touching each other, and never moving or changing in relation to each other. They are in a straight line with each other, and the central Saturn is about three times larger than the outer rings." He also said that Saturn had "ears." In 1612, the rings were horizontal to Earth, when they seemed to disappear. The following year, they reappeared, which further confused Galileo.


Saturn's rings illuminated by the sun.
It was not until 1655 that Christiaan Huygens, using a telescope much more powerful than Galileo's, solved the riddle of the rings. Giovanni Domenico Cassini concluded in 1676 that Saturn's rings were made up of several smaller rings with gaps between them. The largest of these gaps was named Cassini's gap.[9]

Saturn's rings are believed to have been formed from collisions between larger moons, comets, and meteorites, which have remained orbiting the planet due to gravity.

The rings are visible with a fairly modest modern telescope or even with high-quality binoculars. They orbit Saturn's equator at a distance of 6,630–120,700 kilometers. The rings are made up of silicon and iron oxides and ice particles. The particles range in size from dust particles to the size of a small car.

Saturn's moons
Main article: Moons of Saturn
Saturn has 274 known moons (as of 2023). The most recent increase in the number was 128 in 2025.[10][11][12][13][1] The largest of Saturn's moons is Titan, with a diameter of 5,150 km. Titan contains over 90% of the mass orbiting Saturn, including its rings. Cloud-covered Titan is the only moon in the Solar System with a significant atmosphere, mostly composed of nitrogen, and also contains hydrocarbon lakes. Saturn's second-largest moon, Rhea, may have a faint ring system of its own. Most of Saturn's moons are small, icy bodies with low densities. Traditionally, several of Saturn's moons are named after Titans from Greek mythology.

Exploring Saturn
Main article: Exploration of Saturn

An artist's impression of the Cassini spacecraft orbiting Saturn.
Pioneer 11 was the first spacecraft to fly by Saturn in September 1979. Over the next two years, Voyager 1 (in November 1980) and Voyager 2 (in August 1981) flew by the planet. Cassini–Huygens was the first spacecraft to reach Saturn and its moons and ring system from orbit, in 2004. The Huygens probe that accompanied the probe landed on Titan in 2005.

Saturn's rings cannot be seen with binoculars, but an amateur telescope is needed to see them at about 20x magnification. The telescope shows Saturn's largest moon Titan, the second largest moon Rhea, as well as Tethys, Dione, and Iapetus.
