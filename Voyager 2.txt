Voyager 2

Voyager schematic.
Voyager 2 is a space probe of the Voyager space program, launched in 1977. Originally intended to be Mariner 12 of the Mariner space program, Voyager 2 is identical to Voyager 1 (or "Mariner 11"). However, Voyager 2 followed a slightly different trajectory as it passed Saturn, and instead of making a flyby of Titan like Voyager 1, it used Saturn's gravitational pull to travel to Uranus and Neptune. It became the first and so far only space probe to visit the vicinity of those planets, and the first space probe to complete a "Planetary Grand Tour" by visiting Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. This was made possible by the rare relative positions of those four planets, which only occur every 175 years.[1]

Launch

Jupiter
July 9, 1979 – Jupiter flyby.
Saturn
August 25, 1981 – Saturn flyby.

Saturn as imaged by Voyager 2.
While behind Saturn (as seen from Earth), Voyager 2 took radar measurements of Saturn's outer atmosphere to determine variations in its temperature and density. Voyager 2 found that at the top of the atmosphere (70 millibars), Saturn's temperature was 70 kelvin, while at the lowest levels of the measurements (1200 millibars), the temperature rose to 143 kelvin. The north pole was found to be 10 kelvin colder, although this may be due to seasonal variations.

Uranus
January 24, 1986 – Uranus flyby.
Voyager 2 discovered 10 previously unknown moons, studied Uranus' unique atmosphere (Uranus' axial tilt is 97.77°) and its ring system.

Neptune
August 26, 1989 – Neptune flyby.

Neptune as imaged by Voyager 2.
Since Neptune was the last of the planets that Voyager 2 could visit, it was decided to make a close flyby of the moon Triton without worrying about its effect on the spacecraft's orbit, just as Voyager 1 did when studying Saturn and its moon Titan.

The probe also observed a "Great Dark Spot" in Neptune's southern hemisphere, which resembled Jupiter's in appearance and size. The spot appeared to change as the probe passed over it. The spot is not thought to be a storm like Jupiter's, but a hole in Neptune's atmosphere, much like Earth's ozone hole. The Hubble Space Telescope imaged Neptune in 1994, and the spot was no longer visible, although a similar spot had appeared in the planet's northern hemisphere.

Leaving the Solar System
Since the probe's planetary exploration phase is over, it is now aiming to obtain information of interest to NASA about where the Solar System ends (see heliosphere) and what conditions exist in interstellar space outside the Solar System.

As of December 30, 2005, the probe was approximately 78.4 AU from Earth and is moving away from the Solar System at a rate of approximately 3.3 AU per year (approximately 15.6 km/s). The probe has almost left the Solar System. Voyager 2 is expected to continue sending data into the 2020s [2].
