An ultraviolet photograph of Venus taken by the Pioneer Venus Orbiter in 1979 shows the movement of Venus' clouds in the fast winds that circle the planet every four days, creating 90-degree V- and Y-shaped cloud patterns.
Venus (symbol: ♀) is the second planet from the Sun in the Solar System. It appears to Earth as the bright morning and evening star and is the brightest star in the sky. It can be seen even during the day without any aids if you know where to look. Venus orbits the Sun closer than Earth and is the closest planet to Earth.

When viewed through a telescope, Venus often appears as a featureless crescent moon. Only the clouds in the upper atmosphere of the planet are visible through a telescope, where little detail is visible. Venus has been mapped by orbiting space probes equipped with radar. The planet is rich in volcanic formations. The surface of Venus is hot, reaching temperatures of over 460 degrees Celsius, due to the planet's thick carbon dioxide atmosphere, which creates a strong greenhouse effect.[1] Images from space probes that have landed on the planet's surface show rock, stone, and gravel on the surface. The high gas pressure distorts visibility on the planet's surface.

Venus has often been called Earth's sister planet. It is a rocky planet, similar in size and composition to Earth. There are some differences, however, including its slow rotation, its geological formations being in many ways different from those of Earth, and its weak magnetic field.[1]

Venus is named after the Roman goddess of love. Its astronomical symbol is ♀, which is used in other contexts to symbolize woman and femininity.[1]

History
Studies based on climate models suggest that Venus may have been an Earth-like planet with conditions favorable for life about 1–2 billion years ago, when life first appeared on Earth. The relatively high proportion of deuterium in Venus' hydrogen suggests that the planet had large amounts of water; studies suggest that Venus had temperate weather, thick cloud cover, and possibly occasional snowfall.[2] A runaway greenhouse effect has since made Venus the hottest planet in the Solar System.[3]

Venus' slow rotation, which is opposite to Earth's, is thought to be due to a collision with another protoplanet early in the planet's existence.[3]

Gas ring
Venus has a very dense atmosphere, mostly composed of carbon dioxide, which warms the planet through the greenhouse effect. It is thought that Venus's atmosphere is not original, but was created after the early times as a result of volcanic activity.

Venus is estimated to have as much carbon dioxide as Earth, although here it is bound to carbonate rocks in the soil, unlike Venus, where almost all of the carbon dioxide is in the atmosphere. Carbonates are compounds that contain carbon and oxygen. On Earth, it is known that water is needed to form carbonates. Liquid water on Venus could have done the same there, and could have prevented such a drastic greenhouse effect.[4]

The visible cloud tops are at about 70 km, and above them there is haze up to about 90 km. The densest clouds are at about 49–51 km, and a slightly thinner middle layer of clouds below 57 km.[5] Below the clouds there is thin haze at about 30/35–45 km and completely clear on the surface. In some places the clouds are thinner, in others thicker. In ultraviolet light, more cloud features are visible.

The ozone layer, located at an altitude of about one hundred kilometers, is one hundred thousand times thinner than the Earth's layer.[6]

The clouds occur in three distinct layers; the top two consist of small droplets of sulfuric acid and the bottom is probably a phosphoric acid solution. In the uppermost cloud layers, Y-shaped cloud formations thousands of kilometers across can be seen in the ultraviolet region. They are quite short-lived, but are constantly renewed and are thus a permanent feature of the planet's atmosphere. The clouds are quite sparse, and visibility within them is several hundred meters. Visibility on the surface is already several kilometers.

Sulfuric acid in the clouds is produced when water vapor and sulfur dioxide in volcanic eruption gases react with each other under the influence of ultraviolet radiation from the Sun.[7] The Sun's ultraviolet radiation also breaks down the water vapor, which escapes into space.


The structure of Venus' atmosphere and the origin of the greenhouse effect.
The atmosphere is composed of over 96 percent carbon dioxide, 3.5 percent nitrogen, and very small amounts of other elements. The gas pressure at the surface is about 92 times that of Earth's atmosphere, and is equivalent to the pressure in the ocean at a depth of about a kilometer. Much of the hydrogen in Venus' atmosphere is constantly escaping due to temperature alone: this phenomenon is called "thermal escape." Some of the hydrogen that remains in the atmosphere is the hydrogen isotope deuterium, which is slightly heavier than regular hydrogen, so it is not as easily carried away by the solar wind.[4][1]

In September 2020, an international team of researchers published measurements using radio telescopes that showed that the phosphine (PH3) in the Venusian atmosphere contains about 20 parts per billion. The researchers say that no known chemical reaction explains the presence of phosphine on Venus. The researchers suspect that the phosphine may originate from some unknown chemical process or microbial life in the Venusian atmosphere.[8][9] In November 2020, a calibration error was discovered in the measurement. After the error was corrected, the phosphine concentration is now only one part per billion.[10]

The average surface temperature is 464 degrees Celsius,[11] which is higher than the melting point of lead. The temperature never drops below 400 degrees Celsius, and in low-lying areas near the equator the temperature can reach 500 degrees Celsius. The temperature cools quite rapidly as you go upwards: in the upper atmosphere the temperature remains at −45 degrees Celsius.

Due to its very high carbon dioxide content, Venus has an extremely strong greenhouse effect, raising the planet's temperature by about 500 degrees Celsius (1,100 degrees Fahrenheit). A gas-free body at the distance of Venus would have a temperature of about 230 kelvins (-43 degrees Celsius).[4]

Venus may have had a lower temperature billions of years ago before the greenhouse effect got out of control. Venus appears to have some hot spots on its surface, possibly active volcanoes, reaching temperatures of around 700 degrees Celsius.

Venus is even warmer than the atmosphereless Mercury, which orbits the Sun almost half as close and thus receives almost four times as much radiation. The atmosphere reflects 65 percent of visible light and 60 percent of all radiation. The radiation received by Venus is 2,613.9 W/m² in the upper atmosphere and only 1,071.1 W/m² at the surface. Without the greenhouse effect, the atmosphere itself would cool the planet's surface considerably. The surface receives only about 1 percent of the light reaching the upper atmosphere; the light is red-filtered after passing through a layer of clouds and carbon dioxide.

Although Venus's day is slightly longer than a year, causing the same side to face the Sun for a long time, the temperature varies only slightly between the day and night sides of the planet. This is due to the heat-sharing effect of the atmosphere: strong winds in the upper atmosphere, blowing at 300–500 kilometers per hour, circle the entire planet in about four days, distributing the heat received by the day side to the rest of the planet. The wind speed is about one hundred meters per second.

The high wind speed is due to the high radiation energy received by the upper atmosphere. Fast winds are very gusty.

At the surface, wind speeds are low – a few kilometers per hour, but due to the density of the atmosphere, even a small breeze causes considerable resistance.

There is a polar vortex above the polar region of Venus, similar to the one in the upper layers of Earth's atmosphere.

It is currently unclear whether lightning occurs in the atmosphere, as observations from probes that have studied the planet are contradictory.

According to some reports, lightning strikes are possible at volcanoes that are considered active.

Surface

An image of the planet compiled from radar images taken from Venus.
Venus has two large highlands, Ishtar Terra and Aphrodite Terra, which are similar in size to Earth's continents.[1] In addition, the surface has mountains, valleys, and plains. The surface also has volcanic cones, pancake-like uplifts, rift valleys, and large areas of petrified lava. There are both volcanic explosion craters and meteorite impact craters.

Venus's topography is different from that of Earth. There is no clear plate tectonics, or the movement of continental plates, although the landforms near Aphrodite Terra resemble Earth's mid-ocean ridges. Still, the planet's surface appears to be rapidly renewing itself due to intense volcanic activity. Venus' volcanoes are fairly evenly distributed across the planet's surface, and Venus' crust appears to be composed of rock of similar density in every region, since gravity measurements show that the greatest concentrations of gravity are near mountains, unlike on Earth or the Moon. The planet's surface is uniformly distributed in elevation, not divided into continents and oceans as on Earth. About 15 percent of Venus's surface is old highlands, perhaps the planet's original surface; the rest is fairly recent basaltic lava rock. Another classification puts continents at 8 percent, lowlands at 25 percent, and plains at 65 percent.[12] It is believed that Venus's surface experiences periods of intense volcanism every few hundred million years. About 500 million years ago, such a period would have occurred, and the oldest formations on the planet's surface are estimated to be between 800 and 2 billion years old.[13][14]

Surface shapes

Maat Mons, an eight-kilometer-high volcano near the equator. The height ratios have been exaggerated.
Venus's landforms are relatively shallow compared to other terrestrial planets. The difference between the highest and lowest points is about 13 kilometers, compared to about 20 kilometers on Earth. According to measurements by the Pioneer spacecraft, 51 percent of the surface is within 500 meters of the planet's mean radius (6,052 kilometers), and according to results from the Magellan spacecraft, 80 percent of the surface is within one kilometer of the mean radius. Only two percent of the surface is above two kilometers. The highest mountain ranges are located around the Lakshmi Plateau; the most notable of these are the Maxwell Mountains (11–12 km), named after physicist James Clerk Maxwell, the Akna Mountains (7 km), and the Freyja Mountains (7 km). The largest of the highlands is Aphrodite Terra, which is the size of South America, near the equator. Another notable plateau is Ishtar Terra, about the size of Australia, located at about 70° north latitude and also home to the Maxwell Mountains. Because there is no water on Venus, the rocks and mountains are harder than on Earth, and the only erosion is by gentle winds. As a result, the landforms are sharp and steep in many places. With the exception of the Maxwell Mountains and the Alpha Regio and Beta Regio regions, all of Venus' landforms are named after female figures, mostly deities.

Craters
Venus has relatively few impact craters, as the smallest meteorites are usually destroyed upon entering the dense atmosphere or slow down enough on their way to cause no significant impact.[1] Magellan found about 900 impact craters on Venus, very few of which were less than 30 kilometers in diameter. No craters smaller than two kilometers were found. Most of the craters are relatively new and do not occur on top of each other. The craters were often surrounded by molten lava flows at the time of formation, and the lava fields formed by them still appear brighter than the rest of the environment in radar images. The age of the craters on Venus is estimated to be less than 300–500 million years.[15] Some of the craters are volcanic explosion craters.

Fire origin

The 200-kilometer-wide Aine corona, surrounded by small pancake-shaped shield volcanoes.
The surface of Venus is dominated by volcanic activity. Venus does not exhibit the typical plate tectonics of Earth, but about 80 percent of the surface consists of a mosaic pattern of lava plains, with over a hundred separate large shield volcanoes here and there, as well as smaller volcanoes and coronae. The coronae of Venus are volcanic, 100–300 kilometers wide and hundreds of meters high, ring-like formations with depressed centers. They were created when magma from the mantle pushed the crust up. The depression of the centers is a result of the lava cooling and erupting away from the sides of the corona. Coronae are possibly caused by local “hot bubbles”. Volcanic activity is often concentrated in a specific area, but there are also volcanoes that form lava flows all over the planet. Large volcanoes are probably located on so-called hot spots, where hot magma can erupt towards the surface. The fluidity of the lava means that the eruptions have been very calm and that the lava spreads easily, forming a kind of riverbed. It is estimated that there are hundreds of thousands or even millions of small volcanoes on Venus, less than 20 kilometers in diameter. These volcanoes are also shield-shaped and less than a kilometer high. No active volcanoes have been observed so far, but large changes in the sulfur dioxide concentration in the atmosphere indicate their existence.

Other volcanic features on the surface of Venus include novae and arachnoids, which have been found only on Venus. Novae are formed when large amounts of magma erupt from a vent and surrounding fissures, forming radial ridges or depressions when a magma chamber has collapsed below the surface. Arachnoids are spiderweb-like networks of fissures, with radial fissures running between nested ovals. About 250 arachnoids have been found on Venus.

Tectonic activity
Plate tectonics is not observed in the crust of Venus, possibly because the structure of the crust is thinner and weaker than on Earth, which prevents the formation of subduction zones typical of our planet. Instead, the crust forms only local zonal layers. Despite the absence of actual tectonic plates, there are many formations on the surface of Venus that are caused by tectonic activity, such as fold mountains created by volcanic activity, grave rifts, and areas called tesserae that have broken up into a mosaic of plates due to long-term compression. Gravity measurements show that Venus has no asthenosphere, the plastic layer below the crust that allows continental movement, which suggests that surface formations are created directly by mantle convection currents.

Structure

The internal structure of Venus.
Venus closely resembles Earth in its internal structure, as the planets are of the same size and formed in the same region of the Solar System. Beneath the uniform crust is a mantle of molten rock, which makes up most of the planet's volume. At its innermost is an iron core with a diameter of about 6,000 kilometers. Venus' density and diameter are slightly smaller than those of Earth.

Venus' magnetic field is very weak compared to other planets in the Solar System. This may be a result of the planet's very slow rotation period, which means that the molten iron core has not been able to create a proper dynamo that generates a magnetic field. As a result, the solar wind hits Venus' upper atmosphere unhindered. It is thought that Venus originally had as much water as Earth, but when solar wind particles hit the atmosphere, its water molecules broke down into oxygen and hydrogen, with the lighter hydrogen quickly escaping into space. This is why the amount of the heavy isotope of hydrogen, deuterium, is high relative to regular hydrogen. Some of the molecular oxygen combined with atoms in the crust, while some remained in the atmosphere, forming carbon dioxide with carbon.

Appearance

Of all the planets, Venus is the brightest and most visible from Earth, especially in the mornings and evenings. The image of Venus visible was taken from the Pacific Ocean. The second brightest celestial body in the image is Jupiter.
Because Venus is closer to the Sun than the Earth, it is visible at midnight only at the northernmost or southernmost latitudes, at the time of greatest elongation, also in Finland. Depending on which side of the Sun it and the Earth are on, it may be visible at the equator for a maximum of 3–4 hours before sunrise or after sunset: closer to the poles, these daily visibility times may be even longer. North of about 60 degrees latitude, it can be visible throughout the night. When it is near its greatest western elongation, it is visible in the morning sky, when it is also called the morning star. When it is near its greatest eastern elongation, it is visible in the evening sky, when it can be called the evening star (formerly also the evening star). However, it is so bright that when it is near its greatest elongation, it can be seen with the naked eye even in the middle of the day, if you know exactly where to look in the sky.

Venus is normally the third brightest object in the sky after the Sun and Moon. Its apparent magnitude is −4.6 at its brightest, making it considerably brighter than the brightest star Sirius. The bright, star-like Venus may even cast a shadow.

Venus's orbital phases include greatest eastern and western elongation, when Venus appears to be farthest from the Sun on either side of it. At inferior conjunction, Venus is closest to us, in front of the Sun. When Venus is viewed through a telescope, it looks like a large, narrow crescent or is dark. At superior conjunction, it is behind the Sun. When it appears to be a very small full moon, but it is not very noticeable because it is so close to the Sun or even behind the Sun.

During superior conjunction, when Venus is at its farthest from Earth, it disappears from view for several weeks, but during inferior conjunction it passes so quickly that it can be seen perhaps a few days after its disappearance. If Venus is sufficiently above or below the Sun during inferior conjunction, it will still be visible as a very narrow crescent. With powerful binoculars and an amateur telescope, you can see the phases of Venus, but even with a powerful telescope, the details are limited to slight differences in brightness in different parts of the planet. The apparent diameter of Venus is 9–10 arcseconds at superior conjunction and about 60 arcseconds at inferior conjunction.

Rotational properties
The average distance of Venus from the Sun is about 110 million kilometers, or 0.723 AU. Venus orbits the Sun once every 224 days. As seen from Earth, the phases of Venus repeat themselves in a much longer synodic period. The planet's orbit is almost a perfect circle. The elliptical orbit, or eccentricity, is the smallest of the planets, less than 0.7 percent, and the inclination to the Earth is about 3.4 degrees and to the Sun's equator is 3.86 degrees. Compared to the rotation of most other planets, Venus rotates at an angle of three degrees backward, and its rotation period is 243 days.[11] The planet's rotation speed at the equator is small compared to Earth, only 6.52 kilometers per hour.

Venus' retrograde rotation is thought to be caused by a large protoplanet colliding with the planet at a suitable angle during the formation of the Solar System, when there were a few dozen oligarchs, protoplanets about the size of Mars, in the Solar System. Pluto also rotates retrogradely, and Uranus, which lies on its side, rotates either retrogradely or normally, depending on the perspective.

Vicissitudes

Phases of Venus.
Because Venus orbits the Sun inside the Earth's orbit, it has distinct phases, similar to the Moon, when viewed from Earth. When it is at inferior conjunction, closest to Earth, it appears as a very thin crescent in a telescope – the exception being transits, when the planet passes directly in front of the Sun. When it is at its apparent greatest angle to the Sun in elongation (45.0–47.8°), it appears half-illuminated, as an evening star at eastern elongation, and as a morning star at western elongation. When the planet moves to the opposite side of the Sun at superior conjunction, it appears full, but due to its increased distance, it appears more than six times smaller than at inferior conjunction. The cycle of phases lasts one synodic period of Venus, or about 584 days.

Venus is at its brightest on average 37 days before and after inferior conjunction, when 25 percent of the planet's surface is illuminated. At this time, its brightness is −4.6 magnitude. Venus is at its dimmest at superior conjunction, when its brightness drops to −3.5 magnitude.

Overpass

The 2004 overpass.
Main article: Transit of Venus
A transit of Venus occurs when the planet passes directly between the Earth and the Sun in its orbit. The phenomenon is the same as a solar eclipse, but due to Venus's small apparent size, it appears only as a small black disk in front of the Sun.

The transit of Venus is a rare event, as the angle between it and the Earth's orbital plane is 3.4 degrees. It exhibits a certain periodicity, with two transits occurring just under eight years apart, with pairs separated by 121.5 and 105.5 years. Johannes Kepler first predicted a transit in 1631, but it was not observed. The transit, which occurred eight years later, was first observed by Jeremiah Horrocks.

Previous transits occurred on June 8, 2004, and June 6, 2012.[16] They were also visible in Finland. The next transit will occur in 2117, but will not be visible in Finland.

Before modern astronomy, the transit of Venus had important scientific significance. It allowed the Earth's distance from the Sun to be calculated from the parallax, as the beginning of the transit varied slightly when observed from different sides of the Earth. Previously, only the relative distances of the planets had been determined, so the overall picture of the size of the Solar System could finally be formed. In 1761, a bright ring was seen around Venus as it passed outside the edge of the Sun, which led to the conclusion that the planet's atmosphere was refracting light.[17] Similarly, when Venus touched the edge of the Sun from the inside, a dark bulge was seen next to it, which was due to the same reason.

Research
Observations before the space age
Venus is the brightest object in the sky after the Moon and the Sun, so it must have been known since prehistoric times. One of the earliest known astronomical documents is a Babylonian observation table from around 1600 BC, which records the planet's appearance over a period of 21 years. The Babylonians and Sumerians called Venus Dil-bat, Dil-i-pat, or Nin-Dar-Anna, meaning "Mistress of the Sky."[18] In Akkadian, it was the star of the goddess Ishtar, and in China and neighboring cultures, the Metal Star, according to the Chinese philosophy of the five elements.

To the Maya, Venus was the most important celestial body, and they called it Chak ek, 'the great star'. They observed the planet's movements closely, even during the day, as they believed that its position in relation to the other planets influenced the course of life on Earth. They created a religious calendar that represented the full synodic orbit of Venus (about 584 days) in periods of five orbits. To the Aztecs, the god of Venus was Tlahuizcalpantecutli, 'Lord of the House of Twilight' or 'Lord of the Dawn'.

Venus has traditionally been called the Morning Star and the Evening Star, because it is often the most prominent astronomical object in the morning and evening sky.[19] In the word Morning Star, koi means dawn, or the first light of the morning before sunrise. In the earliest times, it may not have been understood that the Morning Star and the Evening Star were the same object. In ancient Greece, the Evening Star was called Hesperos, referring to its appearance in the western sky, and the Morning Star was called Phosphorus. Phosphorus, in Latin Lucifer, literally means light-bringer: when Venus appeared in the eastern sky, she brought with her the light of day. Eventually, it was realized that they were the same object. Diogenes Laertius and Pliny the Elder claimed that Pythagoras was the first to understand this.[20][21] However, copies of early Babylonian clay tablets found in the library of Ashurbanipal have shown that the Babylonians had known this for over a thousand years before the time of Pythagoras.[18]

In ancient and medieval times, the planets and the Sun were assumed to orbit the Earth. However, there was disagreement as to whether Venus and Mercury were closer or further from the Earth than the Sun.[22] According to some writers of late antiquity, Heraclides of Pontus proposed as early as the 4th century BC that Mercury and Venus orbited the Sun instead of the Earth.[23] The theory was not generally accepted in antiquity, but it appeared in several writers in the 14th century.[22]

In modern times, Galileo Galilei made a significant observation when he observed the phases of Venus through his telescope in 1610. This was important evidence for the Copernican heliocentric world view. Galileo also observed that the planet's apparent diameter varied, ranging from its smallest when it was full to its largest when it was a crescent.

In 1932, it was discovered by spectroscopic measurements that the atmosphere of Venus contains carbon dioxide.[24]

As late as the 19th century, it was generally believed that Venus rotates about 24 hours. The slower rotation rate was first proposed by the Italian astronomer Giovanni Schiaparelli, who assumed that Venus's rotation, like that of Mercury, was locked to the period of its revolution. This was confirmed by the observation that Venus always turned in the same direction relative to Earth when it was in inferior conjunction. The assumption was not correct for either planet, but it was quite close. The final information about the rotation period was obtained in the 1960s, when the planet was observed with radar from several observatories starting from the 1961 conjunction. The retrograde nature of its rotation was not noticed until 1964. Venus was found to emit strong radio radiation itself, which indicated a temperature of 300–400 degrees on the planet's surface on the night side. In the early 1960s, various explanations were put forward: the planet was hot due to dust or carbon dioxide floating in the air, or the radiation came from the planet's ionosphere.[25] From 1968 onwards, radar observations showed that Venus had regions that reflected radar radiation in different ways, including Alpha Regio and Beta Regio. In the 1970s, as the spacecraft continued to develop, craters began to appear on the surface.

Space probes
Main article: Space probes that have explored Venus
Space probes have been sent to Venus since the early 1960s. The American Mariner 2 flew by the planet in 1962. The Soviet Union sent many successful probes to Venus. In 1967, the Soviet Venera 4 landed in the Venusian atmosphere to measure temperature and pressure, but it was destroyed before reaching the planet's surface. Only Venera 7 successfully landed on the planet's surface. In 1975, Venera 9 and 10 orbited Venus and dropped capsules that took pictures on the surface of Venus. In 1978, the United States sent two probes called Pioneer Venus to Venus, one of which dropped measurement capsules at different points in the planet's atmosphere and the other mapped a large part of the planet's surface. The Soviet Union continued to send landers to Venus in the 1970s and 1980s. Also notable were the radar survey probes Venera 15 and 16.

The American Magellan orbiter mapped the surface of Venus in greater detail from 1989 to 1994. ESA's Venus Express then operated in orbit from 2006 to 2014.

Venus in science fiction
Before the discovery of Venus by space probes, Venus was considered an Earth-like planet in science fiction. It was often imagined as a wet world covered in rainforests and swamps. In C. S. Lewis's Perelandra, Venus is depicted as an idyllic planet covered in oceans. In Edgar Rice Burroughs' Venus series, the planet, which he calls Amtor, is green and marine, and is protected from sunlight by a permanent cloud cover. Others have imagined Venus as a hot desert, but no one expected temperatures of 480 degrees.

Colonization of Venus
NASA has proposed a plan to build a floating colony in the atmosphere of Venus and create a permanent settlement there. The settlement would be inhabited in airships in orbit at an altitude of about 50 kilometers, where the air pressure and radiation protection are more similar to Earth than on the surface of Venus. According to the plan, expeditions would initially visit Venus' orbit, and then a permanent settlement would be established. Problems with the plan have been seen, for example, filling the airships with helium on Venus and protecting them from sulfuric acid.[26]
