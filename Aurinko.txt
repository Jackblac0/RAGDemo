The Sun (symbol: ☉ ) is the star that the Earth orbits. The planets and other bodies that formed around the Sun form the Solar System. The Sun, like other stars in the Milky Way, orbits the center of the galaxy.

The Sun formed just under five billion years ago, and began producing energy about 4.6 billion years ago. At its current stage, the Sun is a relatively bright star. Its activity varies in cycles of about 11 years. After five billion years, the Sun will have used up all its hydrogen and will begin to transform into a red giant.

Solar radiation is the Earth's most significant source of energy, and the Sun is essential for life on Earth. In ancient times, the Sun was worshipped as a god in many cultures.

Name
The word Aurinko is written with a capital letter when it is used as a proper name for a celestial body. When the word is used for fixed stars in general or as a non-astronomical name (for example, "The sun will shine tomorrow"), the word is written with a lowercase letter.[3][4] The name aurinko has traditionally been known only in the western dialects of Finnish; in the eastern dialects, it has also been equivalent to the Samoyedic[5] language päivä.[6] In the Northern Sámi language, the sun is still called beaivi.[7]

The current name aurinku appears in written sources from the 16th century onwards. The exact origin of the word is unknown. It has been suggested, among other things, to be a variant or loanword of the word auer from some Baltic language (compare Lithuanian aušrà, dawn) or from Germanic languages[8] (Ancient Gutnish ringo, 'ring'). Since the word first appears in Agricola, it has even been suggested to be a neologism created by him, perhaps based on the Latin aurum, gold. In any case, apart from uncertain equivalents in the Sámi languages, there are no cognates in other Finno-Ugric languages.[9][10]

Life cycle
The Sun formed about five billion years ago from interstellar gas, which collapsed into a ball under its own weight. Hydrogen fusion began in the Sun's core about 4.6 billion years ago, and it began to produce energy. After the Sun ignited, it blew away the loose gas from the compacted disk around it, leaving only solid objects and gaseous planets in the Solar System.[11][12]

The Sun is slowly brightening as it ages, as the elemental composition of its core changes as fusion consumes its hydrogen reserves. The Sun is now radiating about 50 percent more intensely than it did 4.5 billion years ago. As the brightening continues, the Earth will heat up so much that in 1–3 billion years the oceans will evaporate, the greenhouse effect will accelerate, and the Earth will become uninhabitable.[12]

After five billion years, the Sun's center will run out of hydrogen, and it will slowly expand and turn red. Gradually, the Sun will become a red giant, engulfing the inner planets Mercury and Venus. The Earth will melt into a lava ball, and some of its rocky core will vaporize into space. At the same time, the Sun will blow its outer layers away into a planetary nebula, exposing the dense core at the center, which will later become a white dwarf. The remaining core of the Sun will slowly cool from a white dwarf to a black dwarf.[13]

Features
The Sun is a G-type main sequence star with a spectral type of G2V. Although the Sun is classified as a yellow dwarf, its true color is white. The Sun is brighter than 85 percent of the other stars in the Milky Way.[14][15] The Sun does not have a binary star like most other stars.[16] The Sun is visible to the naked eye from a distance of about 50 light-years.[17]

Structure
Substance
The temperature in the Sun is so high that substances do not exist in solid, liquid, or gaseous states, but all matter is in the so-called fourth state, plasma.[19]

Energy
The Sun derives its radiant energy from the fusion of hydrogen into helium in the Sun's core. The energy is transferred first as short-wavelength X-rays, then as longer-wavelength X-rays, and finally as convection (heat transfer) to the Sun's surface.[20] The energy must travel through the upper layers of the Sun before being released as sunlight or the solar wind. Estimates of the time it takes for the energy to reach the surface vary from 10,000 to 170,000 years.[21]

Nuclear

The structure of the Sun.
The core is the innermost part of the Sun, where the fusion reactions that produce the Sun's energy take place. Its diameter is about 350,000 kilometers, about a quarter of the Sun's total diameter. The core has a maximum density of 150,000 kg/m3, about 150 times the density of water. The pressure is 225 billion atmospheres.[20] The temperature is about 13,600,000 kelvins, which is considerably hotter than the Sun's surface, which is 5,800 kelvins. Measurements by the SOHO spacecraft indicate that the core rotates faster than the surface.[22]

Except for the final stages of the Sun's life, almost all of the Sun's energy is produced by nuclear fusion reactions, in which hydrogen nuclei, or protons, combine to form helium nuclei, releasing large amounts of energy. Every second, about 600 million tons of hydrogen fuse into 596 million tons of helium. According to the theory of relativity, about 4 million tons of matter is converted into energy per second.[20] The Sun's luminosity is about 385 jottowatts per second.[23] Yet the power density of the Sun's core is only about 277 watts per cubic meter, because a single atom collides with another atom on average only once every five billion years.[24]

Radiation zone
The Sun's core is surrounded by a radiative zone, where nuclear reactions no longer occur. Through it, energy from the core is transferred to the surface by absorption and emission, rising towards the surface of the radiative zone, which is at about 0.7 solar radii[25].[20]

Convection zone
In the convection zone, energy is transferred mainly as convection currents of matter to the surface. The matter in the zone flows towards the surface of the Sun. At the surface, the matter cools and thus becomes denser and sinks back towards the Sun's core.[20]

Surface
The Sun's surface, the photosphere, radiates energy at the speed of light into the surrounding space in all wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation, mainly visible light and infrared radiation, with some ultraviolet radiation. The average temperature of the photosphere is about 5,800 K. The photosphere appears grainy; it has brighter and slightly darker areas. This is due to convection currents coming from deeper in. The 100-kilometer-thick photosphere also contains sunspots, where the temperature is significantly lower.[26] Sunspots resemble uneven holes. The center of a sunspot is the darkest area, with a lighter area around it. The darker color than the rest of the surface is due to the fact that the surface temperature at a sunspot is about 4,500 K, which is about 1,500 degrees cooler than elsewhere. The lower surface temperature is caused by a magnetic field that prevents convection. A typical sunspot is about 10,000 kilometers in diameter and has a lifespan of a few days to months.[27]

Gas ring

The sun's corona, or outer atmosphere, and the atmospheric dispersion zone are visible to the naked eye during a solar eclipse.
The gas sphere is divided into a relatively thin, inner colored ring, the chromosphere, and a far-reaching corona. The entire gas sphere is so thin that it is not visible to the naked eye except during solar eclipses, when the chromosphere appears as a thin red ring around the Sun. The chromosphere is about 2,000 km thick and has a temperature of about 100,000 kelvins.[28][29]

The outermost part of the gas ring, the corona, consists of an even rarer but very hot plasma, with temperatures in places exceeding a million degrees, and it extends outwards by several solar radii. Especially when it is at its most active, the sun's magnetic flares detach material from the corona (including from prominences) in mass ejections, in which plasma is thrown into space at speeds of hundreds of kilometers per second. When the charged particles collide with the Earth's magnetic field a few days later, auroras are created. Charged particles, mainly electrons and protons, released from the coronal plasma move away from it into space at speeds of up to 900 km/s. This phenomenon, called the solar wind, extends through the entire solar system. The part of space within the sphere of influence of its particles and magnetic field is the heliosphere, at whose boundaries, the heliopause at a distance of about 50–100 AU, interstellar space is considered to begin.[28][29]

Activity
Main article: Solar activity

An image of the Sun's surface taken by the Hinode telescope shows the filamentary nature of the plasma.
Solar activity varies in cycles of approximately 11 years. The cycle that appears to be ending in 2018 began in December 2008. At the end of the activity cycle, during the period of minimum activity, there are almost no sunspots.[30]

Sunspots have magnetic fields of up to 0.45 tesla. The magnetic field lines form a loop outside the Sun's surface at two sunspots.[31]

Other surface phenomena include flares, prominences, granulates and so-called sunquakes, which have been observed by ESA's SOHO space probe, among others. The Sun also generates so-called space weather in the Earth's near-Earth space. During periods of peak solar activity, so-called solar storms occur, which are visible on Earth as, for example, powerful and widespread auroras and as power outages on long power lines along the east coast of Canada and the United States.[32]

Radiation
Main article: Solar radiation
The light emitted by the Sun contains all the wavelengths of the colors of the rainbow. When viewed from space, the Sun would appear white. However, from Earth, the Sun often appears yellow because the atmosphere blocks some of the photons emitted by the Sun.[33]

In addition to visible light and infrared radiation, the Sun produces some ultraviolet radiation and small amounts of radio, X-ray, and gamma radiation. Radio radiation comes mainly from the upper atmosphere, as little radiation escapes from the Sun's inner layers. X-ray radiation usually comes from the corona.[34][35]

The importance of radiation for the Earth and humanity

The energy from fusion reactions in the Sun is, in various forms, humanity's most significant source of energy. Of the significant energy sources, only nuclear power, geothermal energy and tidal power do not originate from the Sun.
The Sun is the most important source of energy on Earth, providing almost all of the energy consumed by living organisms through photosynthesis in plants. The physical power of humans also comes from the Sun, as all food, from the plant kingdom to the top of the food chain, represents stored solar energy.[36] With the exception of nuclear power, geothermal energy, and tidal energy, all of the energy consumed by humanity comes from fusion reactions in the Sun through various pathways.[37][38]

Health effects
Solar radiation is a significant source of vitamin D, especially at low latitudes.[39] Sunlight affects the secretion of the darkness hormone melatonin.[40]

Exposure to intense and prolonged sunlight can burn the skin, increasing the risk of melanoma.[41] On the other hand, some studies have found that those who have had the most sunlight exposure during their lifetime are more likely to survive melanoma, suggesting that the sun may also have a protective effect against cancer.[42] You can protect your skin from the sun by avoiding midday sun, wearing protective clothing, and using sunscreen.[43]

Looking at the Sun with the naked eye is dangerous to the eyes. The Sun can be observed safely with, for example, eclipse glasses or by using a filter with a telescope or binoculars.[44][45][46]

Research
The Sun is studied scientifically using a wide variety of instruments. Telescopes detect visible light, infrared radiation, and radio wavelengths. A spectrograph can be used to disperse the Sun's light into different wavelengths, which reveals, for example, the chemical composition of the Sun's gas. A coronagraph studies the Sun's corona.[47] Solar probes study the Sun from close range. The closest ever sent to the Sun was NASA's Parker Solar Probe, which passed within 24 million kilometers of the Sun in April 2019.[48]

In culture
In religion
Main article: Sun worship

Ra, the sun god of the ancient Egyptians.
The Sun has been worshipped as a god in many cultures, either as such or in the form of a human or animal.[49] The Sun was considered to be closely connected to everyday life and to influence people's lives. The Sun was worshipped as the Great Father, while the Moon was worshipped as the Great Mother. In the Middle East, for example, the Sun was worshipped in general in ancient times, and Sun worship was central to ancient Egyptian religion.[50] Some Native Americans considered the Sun to be a god. For example, the Incas had a sun god named Inti.[51] Some Plains Indians had sun dances.[52] The ancient Finns did not have a sun god, but the Lapps made sacrifices to the Sun so that it would shine.[53]

In mythology
Ancient cultures invented various explanations for solar eclipses, such as an attack by a dragon or angry gods. For the ancient Norse, the end of the world, Ragnarök, began with a solar eclipse. Because the phenomenon was not understood, it was often feared.[54]

In construction
Since ancient times, people have observed the rising and setting points of the Sun on the horizon, as well as the days when the Sun was at its highest or lowest at noon during the year. Some ancient temples and other buildings, such as the Temple of Karnak and Stonehenge, are precisely aligned with some of these points. About half of the Finnish megalithic churches have solar orientations.[55]
