Official website

Voyager schematic diagram
Voyager 1 is an unmanned spacecraft launched on September 5, 1977, to study the outer planets of the Solar System and interstellar space. It is still operational at the moment (2026) and is expected to operate until the end of its fourth decade. It is the farthest human-made object from Earth. Voyager 1 has passed the outermost sphere of influence of the Solar System (see heliosphere), that is, it has reached interstellar space. The second space probe of the Voyager space program was Voyager 2, which is identical to Voyager 1.

At a distance of 21 billion kilometers (141 AU) from the Sun, Voyager 1 has reached the heliopause, the region of the Solar System after the termination shock that marks the transition from the Solar System to interstellar space. At this distance, it takes Voyager 1's radio signal more than 22 hours to reach the Jet Propulsion Laboratory's control center. The probe is on a hyperbolic trajectory and has reached escape velocity, meaning it will not re-enter the Solar System. Like Pioneer 10, the decommissioned Pioneer 11, and its sister probe Voyager 2, Voyager 1 is becoming an interstellar probe.

Voyager 1's mission was to study Jupiter and Saturn, their moons and ring systems; its current mission is to detect the heliopause and measure particles in the solar wind and interstellar matter.

Each Voyager probe is powered by three radioisotope thermal generators (RTGs), which have greatly exceeded their calculated lifespan and were expected to provide sufficient power for a radio transmitter communicating with Earth until around 2020.

Voyager 1 carries a collection of golden records (including the rock 'n' roll song Johnny B. Goode by Chuck Berry) and a vinyl record player.[1] The records contain direct coordinates to our planet Earth, engravings of male and female figures, and photographs, musical pieces, and audio greetings in various Earth languages. They also include recordings of natural sounds, such as thunder, wind, and rain. They also include recordings of Igor Stravinsky's The Rite of Spring, a greeting from U.S. President Jimmy Carter, and Blind Willie Johnson's gospel blues song Dark Was the Night. They also include music from various nations and tribes, such as Navajo singing, Indian raga, and Solomon Islands panpipes, as well as recordings in 55 different languages greeting potential alien civilizations.[2]

The probe has taken an image of Earth known as the Pale Blue Dot.[3]

Flight plan and launch

Voyager routes.
Voyager 1 was originally planned as the Mariner 11 probe of the Mariner space program. It was designed to exploit a new technology of its time, the gravitational pull of planets. Coincidentally, the development of interplanetary probes coincided with the once-in-175-year "Planetary Grand Tour," a series of successive planetary gravitational pulls that allow a single probe to visit all four of the Solar System's gas giant planets: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune, using only minimal fuel for course corrections. The identical Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 probes were designed with the grand tour in mind and their launches were timed to allow for that trip if desired.

NASA launched Voyager 1 on September 5, 1977, from Launch Complex 41 at Cape Canaveral Air Force Station aboard a Titan IIIE Centaur rocket, shortly after its sister spacecraft, Voyager 2. Although Voyager 1 launched after its sister spacecraft, it was sent into a faster orbit so that it would reach Jupiter and Saturn before its sister spacecraft.

At launch, the Titan IIIE launch vehicle's second stage failed to burn for about a second, leaving a similar amount of fuel unused. Flight control suspected that Voyager 1 would not reach Jupiter, but the launch vehicle's next stage had enough fuel to compensate for the short burn of the previous stage.

Jupiter flyby

Detail of Jupiter's atmosphere as imaged by Voyager 1.
Voyager 1 began photographing Jupiter in January 1979. It was closest to Jupiter on March 5, 1979, at 349,000 kilometers from the center of the planet.

The close distance allowed for greater observational accuracy. Most of the observations of the moons, ring systems, magnetic fields, and radiation were made in the days before and after the flyby. The planet's photography ended in April.

Both Voyager probes made important new observations of Jupiter and its moons. The most surprising of these was the volcanic activity on Io, which had not previously been observed from Earth or during flybys by Pioneer 10 and Pioneer 11.

Saturn flyby
The gravitational pull of Jupiter worked as planned, and the probe headed for Saturn, which flew by on November 12, 1980, at a distance of 124,000 km above the planet's cloud tops. The probe observed the complex structure of Saturn's rings and studied the atmospheres of Saturn and its moon Titan. Because Titan had a thick atmosphere, Voyager flight control at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory decided to direct the probe to fly close to Titan and abort the "major planetary orbit." (For more on the continuation of the major planetary orbit, see the Uranus and Neptune sections in the article Voyager 2, which completed the major orbit.)

The additional gravitational pull from the Titan flyby took Voyager 1 beyond the plane of the planets, ending its planetary exploration phase. It is estimated that both Voyager probes will have enough power to operate at least some of their instruments until 2020.

Heliopause

Voyager 1 in the heliosheath.
See also: heliosphere
As Voyager 1 heads into interstellar space, its instruments will continue to observe the Solar System; JPL scientists will use plasma wave instruments on Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 to study the heliopause.

Voyager 1's solar wind detector ceased operation in 1990, and its exact position relative to the heliosphere is unknown. However, in May 2005, NASA announced in a press release that scientists had reached a consensus that Voyager 1 was now in the heliosheath.[4]

Distance traveled
On August 15, 2006, the probe reached a distance of 100 AU (about 15 billion kilometers) from Earth, making it the farthest human-made object from Earth. Voyager 1 was calculated to be 16.67 billion kilometers from Earth on October 21, 2009.[5] Voyager 1 is moving away from the Solar System at a rate of about 3.6 AU per year (about 17.2 km/s), faster than Voyager 2. Voyager 1 is not headed towards any particular star, but even if the probe were headed towards the nearest star, Alpha Centauri, it would take about 80,000 years to reach it. The home page of NASA's Voyager website has a real-time distance calculator for the Voyager pair.

On September 12, 2013, NASA announced that the 36-year-old Voyager 1 had already traveled 12 billion miles (19 billion km) from the Sun, entering interstellar space, or the outer reaches of our Solar System. Based on the measurements, the research team determined that it had left the Solar System on August 25, 2012.[6][7][8] This is the greatest achievement in space for humanity since the Moon landing, according to scientists. This was confirmed when the probe sent back new data that indicated that the probe had moved out of the Solar System.
