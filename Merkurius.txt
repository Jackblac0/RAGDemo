Mercury (symbol: ☿ ) is the smallest planet in the Solar System and the closest to the Sun. Its orbital period around the Sun is the shortest of the planets: 88 days. It rotates on its axis every 59 days. Mercury is a dry, lifeless, and almost gas-free terrestrial rocky planet. Its surface, filled with impact craters, resembles the surface of the Moon. Due to the lack of a gas atmosphere, the planet's temperature fluctuates greatly: they range from night temperatures of about 100 K (−173 °C) to daytime temperatures of 700 K (427 °C).

Mercury is one of the brightest objects in the sky and has been known since prehistoric times. Its name comes from the Roman mythology Mercurius , who was the god of thieves and merchants and the messenger of the gods.

Orbit
Mercury's orbital period around the Sun is 88 days, and its orbital period is 59 days. The ratio of the orbital period to the orbital period is therefore 2:3, meaning that Mercury is said to rotate in a 2:3 resonance with its orbital period. The variation in the times of day is 176 days. Mercury's axial tilt is only 0.01 degrees, meaning that the planet is almost vertical in relation to its orbital plane.

For a long time, it was thought that the planet always kept the same side facing the Sun, because the few possible moments of observation occurred when the planet was in the same position in spring and autumn.

Mercury's orbit around the Sun deviates considerably from a circular orbit. The eccentricity, or ovality, of the orbit has varied between 0.0 and 0.47 over millions of years. The ovality is now 0.206 and Mercury's distance from the Sun varies between 0.31 AU and 0.47 AU. The planet's mean distance is 0.387 AU.

Mercury's orbit is so strongly affected by its proximity to the Sun that general relativity is required to calculate its orbit. The perihelion of the orbit was known as early as the 19th century. Most of the deviation is due to perturbations from other planets, but it was found to be 43 arcseconds larger than those perturbations could have predicted. Before the theory of relativity, this deviation was thought to be due to the influence of an unknown planet, Vulcan , orbiting inside Mercury's orbit.

Mercury has no moons.[4]

Physical properties
Gas ring
Mercury's atmosphere is extremely thin, and its magnetic field is very weak. The gas ring is formed from surface material blown away by the solar wind. The gas ring consists mostly of helium, sodium, and oxygen, with small amounts of hydrogen and potassium. Mercury also has a long sodium tail that extends into space, which was discovered in 2001. The pressure in the gas ring is about two millionths of that of Earth.

Surface shapes
Mercury is a slow-rotating inner planet. Due to its long rotation period and lack of atmosphere, the temperature on Mercury's surface varies by hundreds of degrees depending on whether it is on the day or night side. The surface temperature varies between 90 and 740 kelvins (−173 and +467 °C), and averages 452 kelvins (179 °C). The Sun is on average 2.5 times as large as it is on Earth, but this apparent diameter varies considerably because Mercury's orbit is elliptical. The Sun is 6.5 times brighter than on Earth from Mercury, and the solar irradiance there is 9.13 kW/m². The Sun exerts a tidal force on Mercury that is 17 percent greater than the tidal force exerted by the Moon on Earth.

Only about 45 percent of Mercury's surface has been mapped before 2008. Craters are the most striking feature of Mercury's surface. They come in various sizes. The largest of the impact craters, the 1,500 km-diameter Caloris Basin, is a ring-shaped formation resembling the lunar lava fields, or "seas". Its rim mountains rise to a height of two kilometers. On the opposite side of the Caloris Basin, the crust has been broken by the shock waves of the impact reflecting off the planet, over an area about a hundred kilometers in diameter. Mercury also has mountains, valleys, ridges, cliffs and plateaus, as well as areas with different reflections. Craters often emit radial streaks. The ridges are up to hundreds of kilometers long and the mountains up to about three kilometers high. The mountains are thought to have been formed when Mercury shrank by 0.1 percent. Mercury may have had volcanoes, but they are likely more than a billion years old.[5] Craters often show numerous young and old small depressions, thought to have been formed by the eruption of volatiles from below the surface.[6][7][8]

Measurements by the Messenger spacecraft indicate that there is ice at the bottom of craters on Mercury, which are always in shadow. The ice is covered by a layer of regolith that prevents the ice from evaporating into space. It has been estimated that Mercury has as much ice as one-thousandth the amount of ice in Earth's Antarctic.[5]

Mercury is only 1.4 times the diameter of Earth's Moon, and is now the smallest official planet, after Pluto was removed from the list of celestial bodies that can be defined as planets. Its mass is 6 percent of Earth's mass. The planet is smaller than Saturn's moon Titan, for example. Mercury's density is 5.43 g/cm3, making it one of the densest planets. The Moon, which resembles Mercury, has a density of only 3.34 g/cm3. For comparison, the density of Earth in an incompressible state would be 4.0 g/cm3.

Mercury is believed to have a huge iron core, which produces a magnetic field about 1 percent of that of Earth. The field is tilted 7 percent relative to the axis of rotation. Another source of magnetism may be relic magnetism. The iron core makes up 60–70 percent of the planet's mass, and the silicate crust and mantle together make up 30 percent. It is estimated that the radius of Mercury's core is 1,800–1,900 kilometers, and the thickness of the silicate mantle is 500–600 kilometers. This makes the radius of the iron core 75 percent of the planet's radius.

The iron core covers 42% of the planet's volume, compared to 17% for Earth. Earth is slightly denser than Mercury because it is more compressed due to its 20 times greater mass. Mercury's large iron core may be the result of a large object colliding with the planet at high speed during the formation of the Solar System, throwing off part of the planet's silicate mantle. The mass of this impactor would have been approximately one-sixth that of Mercury. Other researchers suggest that the early Sun heated Mercury to high temperatures and vaporized parts of it.

Research
Main article: Exploration of Mercury

Mercury as imaged by Mariner 10 in 1974–1975
Maps of Mercury have been made since the 19th century, but these are inaccurate.

It was relatively difficult to send a space probe to Mercury because it is even closer to the Sun than Venus. This causes thermal design problems for space probes and radiation tolerance problems for their electronics. Mercury's location is also difficult in terms of the orbit of a research probe. The planet has not interested researchers or funders as much as, say, Mars, Venus, and Saturn.

Mariner 10 spacecraft flew by Mercury three times in 1974–1975. Unfortunately, each flyby occurred when Mercury was in approximately the same position relative to the Sun, and only 40–45 percent of the surface was photographed.

In 2004, NASA launched a space probe called Messenger, which flew past the planet three times in 2008–2009 and is scheduled to orbit it in 2011. Messenger's tasks include photographing previously unphotographed areas of the planet.

BepiColombo probe, jointly developed by ESA and the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency , was launched in 2018 and will enter Mercury's orbit in late 2025. Before that, the probe will make a few close passes that will be used for orbit correction.

Detection in Finland
In Finland, Mercury can be observed four to five times a year, but only once or twice in the spring evening sky and in the autumn morning sky when the planet happens to be farthest from the Sun, at the same time that the ecliptic forms the largest angle with respect to the horizon. Mercury's so-called greatest elongation is about 20 degrees, and at that time the planet is visible for about three weeks. When Mercury is at its farthest elongation in summer or winter, it is poorly visible, because the ecliptic is at a small angle with respect to the horizon. If the planet happens to be at western elongation in spring or eastern elongation in autumn, it is not visible from Finland at all, because the Sun either rises before the planet in the morning or the planet sets before the Sun in the evening. This happens even if Mercury is on the side of the Sun seen from Finland that it travels "behind" the Sun in the evening and "ahead" of it in the morning.

The apparent brightness changes strongly during the visibility period. In its eastern elongation, Mercury is brighter at the beginning of its visibility period, and vice versa in its western elongation. Mercury is visible to the naked eye, but it is most easily seen with binoculars. The phases of Mercury can be seen with a telescope. It is also possible to observe the planet from the daytime sky with a telescope. This eliminates atmospheric disturbances that normally hinder the observation of the planet from the low evening or morning sky, as well as, of course, the limitations on its visibility caused by Mercury's elongation. Unlike Venus, Mercury is, however, very difficult to find in the bright daytime sky. A computer-controlled so-called goto telescope is a great help in finding it.
